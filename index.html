The internet wasn’t built for trust, democracy, or public service.
Dot-Public is an attempt to change that — structurally, not symbolically.
For decades, civic institutions have relied on an internet driven by commercial incentives: engagement, data harvesting, profit. As a result, public trust is eroding, and the digital commons feels fragile.

Dot-Public proposes a new approach: a dedicated civic layer of the internet. A place where institutions serve the public without hidden motives. Where people know they’re in a trusted space. Where privacy and democratic resilience are built into the infrastructure itself.

Why Dot-Public Exists
Public institutions — libraries, universities, broadcasters, government agencies — hold society together. Yet online, they’re forced to operate on platforms whose rules and revenue models often contradict public service.

Dot-Public is a structural fix:
  • A top-level domain (.public) dedicated to civic actors.
  • Strict rules for participation, transparency, and privacy.
  • Shared infrastructure that makes public digital services cheaper, safer, and more sustainable.
The goal is not to regulate the existing internet from the outside, but to build a civic alternative within it.
A Digital Public Space You Can Trust
Imagine an internet where:
  • You know instantly that you’re in a trusted civic space.
  • Public services operate free from commercial manipulation.
  • No silent tracking or hidden data harvesting occurs.
  • Knowledge and records remain available for future generations.
  • Civic actors can collaborate across borders without cost barriers.
Dot-Public aims to make this real — not through slogans, but through infrastructure, standards, and enforceable rules.
The Core Ingredients of Dot-Public
Dot-Public isn’t just a new domain name. It’s a new piece of civic infrastructure, built on five pillars:
  1. The .public Domain
    A permissioned top-level domain where only verified civic actors can publish. It acts as the street sign for the digital public space.
  2. A Civic Rubric
    A rulebook defining who may participate, what behaviours are allowed, and how trust is earned and maintained.
  3. Privacy-First Technology
    Tools like enclaves, mixnets, and zero-knowledge proofs to ensure services operate without silent surveillance or hidden data trails.
  4. Provenance and Permanence
    Standards for digital signatures, metadata, and permanent records — so civic information remains verifiable and preserved.
  5. Shared Civic Tools
    Open-source services for search, maps, identity, and communication — free from commercial surveillance and vendor lock-in.
Dot-Public aims to build an ecosystem that public institutions can use without compromising their civic mission.
